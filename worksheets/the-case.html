Why trunk-based?

the case, before the ladders
1 of 2 · the why
The whole idea: everyone merges small changes into one shared branch — trunk — at least once a day. Not long-lived side branches merged weeks later. That's it.

✗ the old way hurts

  • Long-lived branches drift → painful, scary merges.
  • Big releases bundle changes → when it breaks, which one?
  • Finished work waits in a review queue → done, not delivered.
  • Feedback arrives next sprint, when it's expensive to fix.

✓ the payoff

  • Small batches → easy to review, safe to ship, quick to fix.
  • Real continuous integration → conflicts caught in minutes, not weeks.
  • Deploy decoupled from release → ship now, switch on when ready.
  • Feedback today → learn while it's cheap to change.
// two reasons to do this now
1 · it's measured, not taste

The DORA research finds trunk-based development predicts elite delivery — the four outcomes every org wants:

2 · it's the foundation for AI

Applied AI amplifies whatever workflow you already have. An agent makes a tower of changes faster than anyone can review — so AI pays off only on a workflow that already absorbs volume safely:

parallel agents in their own worktrees · AI features shipped dark behind a flag · a fast CI gate as the check, not per-keystroke review.

// the worries you're already thinking
Commit straight to trunk? Reckless? — No. Changes are small, hidden behind a flag till ready, and a fast CI gate (<10 min) catches breakage early.
No PR review — how keep quality? — Review moves from a queue to real time: pair/mob on hard things, post commits, lean on CI. (Kit · ⑤)
What about half-finished work? — It ships dark behind a flag: live in prod, invisible to users. (Kit · ②)

The cost of the queue

what a PR-heavy flow really buys — and bills
2 of 2 · the why
6 hrs
One engineer. One day. Six hours in pull requests. A traditional read calls that diligence. In flow terms it's a bill — and the team pays it every single day. // overheard on a real team, this week

feels like

Control. Quality. Oversight. A gate every change must earn its way through.

actually is

Inventory + latency. Finished work that can't be used yet, waiting on a human queue — while the meter runs on engineer time.

// the hidden taxes on the queue
Engineer hourstime in the queue — authoring, waiting, re-reviewing — × the team × every day.
Lead timecode done Monday, merged Thursday = three days of value sitting idle.
Context-switchingfinish → wait → return to cold review comments. Focus fragments.
Rubber-stampingbig PRs tire reviewers, so they wave them through. The queue bills for quality it doesn't deliver.
Throughput ceilingdelivery rate is capped by review latency, not coding speed.
// what a Head of Engineering will ask
PRs are our quality gate. — The CI gate is — automated, on every change. Real-time review (pair/mob) covers the hard parts. The async queue adds latency, not assurance.
How do I keep oversight? — Every commit on trunk is tested, deployable, and posted for visibility. More signal, sooner — not less.
Isn't committing to trunk riskier? — Smaller changes + flags + fast recovery → lower change-fail rate. In DORA, speed and stability rise together.
My seniors review to mentor juniors. — Pairing and mobbing mentor better and faster than cold async comments.
The bottom line. A traditional lead optimises the review. Trunk-based optimises the flow: make changes small enough that review is synchronous or seconds, gate with CI, ship dark. Same quality, a fraction of the cost — and the data says teams that drop the queue ship more often and fail less.
